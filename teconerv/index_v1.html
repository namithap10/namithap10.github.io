Abstract

Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) have recently demonstrated impressive performance for video compression. However, since a separate INR must be overfit for each video, scaling to high-resolution videos while maintaining encoding efficiency remains a significant challenge. Hypernetwork-based approaches predict INR weights (hyponetworks) for unseen videos at high speeds, but with low quality, high compressed size, and prohibitive memory needs at higher resolutions. We address these fundamental limitations through three key contributions: (1) an approach that decomposes the weight prediction task spatially and temporally, breaking short video segments into patch tubelets to reduce the memory overhead by 20× during pretraining; (2) a residual-based storage scheme that captures only the differences between consecutive segment representations, significantly reducing bitstream size; and (3) a temporal coherence regularization framework that encourages changes in the weight space to be correlated with changes in video content. Our proposed method, TeCoNeRV, achieves substantial improvements of 2.47dB and 5.35dB PSNR over the baseline at 480p and 720p on UVG, with 36% lower bitrates and 1.5-3× faster encoding speeds. With our low memory usage, we are the first hypernetwork-based approach to demonstrate results at 480p, 720p and 1080p on UVG, HEVC, and MCL-JCV.

Method

TeCoNeRV addresses the fundamental scaling limitations of hypernetwork-based video compression through three complementary innovations:

Method Figure Placeholder
Figure 2 from paper: Overview showing patch-tubelets, residual encoding, and temporal coherence finetuning

1

Patch-Tubelet Decomposition

We partition videos into small spatio-temporal volumes, making memory requirements independent of video resolution. This enables the first hypernetwork-based results at 720p and 1080p.

2

Residual Encoding

Instead of storing full weights per clip, we store only the first clip's weights and compact residuals for subsequent clips, exploiting temporal redundancy.

3

Temporal Coherence Regularization

We regularize weight transitions to be smooth over time, producing smaller residuals that compress more efficiently. The regularization strength provides direct rate control.

Temporal Regularization Strength

Varying λtemp provides up to 18% bitrate reduction with only 4% PSNR loss, offering interpretable rate control.

Overlapping Inference: To eliminate boundary artifacts from patch-based reconstruction, we employ strided inference with overlap. Cropping at patch boundaries produces smooth transitions with minimal computational overhead.

Resolution-Independent Training

Key Insight: A single model trained on 480p videos can perform high-quality inference at 720p and 1080p — no high-resolution training data required.

Our patch-tubelet approach learns to predict weights for fixed-size spatio-temporal regions, making resolution scaling a function of patch count rather than model capacity. This is fundamentally impossible for prior hypernetwork methods, which are tied to a fixed resolution and whose memory constraints prevent high-resolution training.
